# Usual Brewing Method

This summarises the established home-brewing method documented in `Simple All-Grain Beer Brewing.pdf`. The original PDF remains outside this repository. Use these constraints when proposing or adapting brewing recipes.

## Principles

- Keep the process simple, inexpensive and close to a traditional country craft.
- Brew all-grain rather than from malt-extract tins.
- Aim for batches of approximately 20-30 L.
- Avoid unnecessary additives and processing aids. In particular, finings such as isinglass or Irish moss are not normally used.
- Accept the natural haze and sediment associated with bottle-conditioned beer.

## Equipment

- One 30 L stainless-steel stockpot serves as both mash tun and boiler.
- The stockpot has a tap, hose and a simple internal filter made from stainless-steel flexible-hose mesh.
- Wort runs into a separate fermentation vessel before the stockpot is cleaned and reused for the boil.
- Cooling is simple rather than equipment-heavy; historically, the covered fermentation vessel was cooled in a water butt.
- Reusable swing-top bottles are preferred.

The handwritten ingredient note describes the mash-pot capacity as 20-30 L. Plan recipes conservatively around the 30 L vessel and leave safe headspace during the boil.

## Mash And Boil

1. Heat approximately 30 L of water to boiling, then cool it to the recipe's mash temperature. The traditional baseline is approximately 66°C.
2. Add the crushed grain and mash for approximately one hour unless the recipe specifies otherwise.
3. Run the wort into the fermentation vessel. Sparge only when useful for the recipe and vessel capacity.
4. Remove the spent grain, rinse the stockpot, return the wort to it and bring it to a rolling boil.
5. Boil for approximately one hour, using early additions for bitterness, additions in the final 10-15 minutes for flavour and additions near flameout for aroma.
6. Transfer the wort immediately after the boil and cool it promptly to the pitching temperature appropriate for the yeast.

## Fermentation

- Ale is normally fermented at ambient household temperature.
- WHC Lab Saturated is the currently available top-cropping English ale yeast.
- Fermentation has historically taken approximately 5-10 days, with bottling often around day seven. For each batch, confirm that fermentation is complete before bottling rather than relying on elapsed time alone.
- Yeast may be cropped cleanly and carried into a later batch. If dry hopping, crop the yeast before adding hops to reduce hop debris in the saved culture.

## Bottle Conditioning

1. Clean and sanitise reusable swing-top bottles and siphoning equipment carefully. Sanitation is particularly important after primary fermentation.
2. Confirm fermentation is complete before bottling. Premature bottling or excessive priming sugar can create dangerous pressure.
3. Siphon the beer into bottles, leaving suitable headspace.
4. Reserve enough of the final beer to dissolve the priming sugar or honey into a pourable batch mixture.
5. Dose each bottle with a measured amount of the mixture. The historical method used approximately 5 ml of mixture per bottle, delivered with a 20 ml syringe.
6. Seal, label with at least a batch identifier and condition in a protected storage box.
7. Allow at least 1-2 weeks before drinking; longer conditioning is often beneficial.

The historical range was 1/2-1 1/2 teaspoons of demerara sugar or honey per bottle. This is too broad to use as an automatic recipe instruction because bottle volume, beer temperature, residual carbonation and desired carbonation all affect a safe dose. Future recipes should state a conservative, calculated total priming mass for the actual finished volume, then dissolve that amount in a small quantity of beer and divide the mixture evenly between bottles.
